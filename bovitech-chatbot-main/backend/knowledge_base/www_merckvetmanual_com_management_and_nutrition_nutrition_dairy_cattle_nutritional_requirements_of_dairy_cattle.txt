Definition
Mastitis is an inflammation of the mammary gland, usually caused by infection when bacteria enter through the teat canal.

Types of mastitis

1. Subclinical mastitis
- No visible signs
- Milk appears normal
- Detected using somatic cell count (SCC)
- SCC ≥ 200,000 cells/mL indicates infection
- Causes reduced milk production

2. Clinical mastitis
- Visible abnormal milk (clots, color change)
- Swollen, hot, painful udder
- May include fever and loss of appetite

Severity:
- Mild: only milk changes
- Moderate: udder inflammation
- Severe: systemic signs (shock, fever)

Main causes
- Bacteria are the primary cause:
  - Staphylococcus aureus
  - Streptococcus agalactiae
  - Streptococcus spp
  - Coliforms (E. coli, Klebsiella)

Transmission

1. Contagious (during milking)
- Milkers’ hands
- Milking machines
- Infected cows

2. Environmental
- Dirty bedding
- Mud, manure, water
- Flies
- Poor hygiene

Risk factors
- Dirty or injured teats
- Poor milking hygiene
- Wet or contaminated environment
- Early lactation or after calving
- Weak immune system

Signs to detect mastitis

Check the cow:
- Fever
- Low appetite

Check the udder:
- Swelling
- Heat
- Pain
- Redness

Check the milk:
- Clots
- Watery appearance
- Color change

Diagnosis
- Strip test (before milking)
- California Mastitis Test (CMT)
- Somatic Cell Count (SCC)
- Milk culture (most accurate)

Treatment

General rules:
- Treat early
- Consult veterinarian
- Identify pathogen if possible

Methods:
- Intramammary antibiotics
- Systemic antibiotics (severe cases)
- Anti-inflammatory drugs
- Fluid therapy (for severe mastitis)

Important:
- Respect withdrawal periods (milk/meat)
- Discard milk during treatment

Dry cow treatment
- Best time to treat subclinical mastitis
- Higher cure rates
- Prevents new infections

Methods:
- Antibiotic infusion at drying-off
- Teat sealants

Prevention

Milking hygiene:
- Wash and dry udders
- Use clean towels (one per cow)
- Wash hands or wear gloves
- Milk healthy cows first, infected last

Teat dipping:
- Essential after each milking
- Use disinfectant (iodine, chlorhexidine)

Environment:
- Clean and dry bedding
- Good ventilation
- Control flies

Management:
- Maintain milking equipment
- Avoid teat injuries
- Monitor cows regularly

Monitoring
- Regular SCC testing
- Keep records of infections and treatments

Culling
- Remove cows with chronic or repeated mastitis

Economic impact
- Reduced milk production
- Poor milk quality
- Veterinary costs
- Milk rejection due to antibiotics

Key takeaway
- Mastitis is one of the most important diseases in dairy farming
- Early detection and hygiene are critical
- Prevention is more effective than treatment
