Husbandry Unit 10.5: Mastitis

Mastitis is an inflammation of the mammary glands and may be acute or chronic.

How can you know if your cow has mastitis?
You should know:
- the signs
- the tests

Why do cows get mastitis?
If the teats are not healthy and clean, bacteria may enter and cause mastitis.

How can you treat and prevent mastitis?
- always call the veterinarian if one of your cows has mastitis
- make sure your cows have good health, hygiene, and housing

The cost of mastitis
In many dairy countries, mastitis may be the most costly disease of the dairy industry.
Obvious costs include:
- veterinary treatment and drugs
- lost production from affected udders
- withdrawal time for milk and meat due to antibiotic residues after treatment

There are also hidden costs:
- cows with subclinical mastitis may have low milk production
- milk from cows with mastitis is lower in quality
- milk contains more cells and less lactose, fat, protein, and other desirable substances
- dairy payment may decrease

Mastitis in buffalo
In lactating herds, mastitis is of major importance.

Why is mastitis important?
- mastitis costs money
- veterinary fees and medicines increase
- low-quality milk brings less income
- milk with antibiotics may be rejected
- all forms of mastitis reduce milk production

Definition of mastitis
Mastitis cases can be grouped according to symptoms.

Types of mastitis

Acute mastitis
- poor general condition
- high fever
- low feed intake
- udder is hot, swollen, hard, painful
- milk is abnormal, watery, thin, with flecks or clots, yellow or brownish

Chronic mastitis
Chronic hidden mastitis:
- cow and milk may appear normal
- less milk
- poor milk quality

Chronic mild mastitis:
- glands slightly hard, swollen, painful
- milk slightly abnormal, watery, discolored

Chronic mastitis with gland shrinkage:
- scar tissue replaces gland tissue
- gland becomes hard and shrinks
- no milk production from the gland

The strip test
The strip test should be performed before each milking because:
- it helps detect clinical mastitis
- it flushes out bacteria in the teat canal
- it stimulates milk let-down

Use a strip cup and clean it between each milking.

How can you know if your cow has mastitis?
Check the cow:
- fever
- low feed intake

Check the udder:
- swollen
- hard
- red
- hot
- painful

Check the milk:
- milk a few streams from one teat into the strip cup
- examine carefully
- clean the strip cup
- repeat for the next teat

California Mastitis Test (CMT)
CMT can detect subclinical mastitis.
It measures somatic cells in the milk.
It gives a rough indication of cell count, but laboratory counts are more exact.

Other factors such as age, stage of lactation, teat injury, stress, or other disease can also affect cell count.

Agents causing mastitis
Infection with bacteria or fungi is the usual cause.
The normal route of infection is through the teat canal and then through the mammary gland.

Common bacteria include:
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Streptococcus agalactiae
- other streptococci
- coliform bacteria
- Pseudomonas
- Corynebacterium pyogenes

Streptococcus agalactiae survives mainly in the cow udder, so it can be eradicated if all infected cows are treated.
Other bacteria survive in the environment, on milking tools, and on hands, so hygiene is essential.

Why do cows get mastitis?
In healthy teats, few bacteria enter because:
- the skin protects against bacteria
- the teat opening is tight
- a waxy substance seals the opening

If the teat is unhealthy, injured, or very dirty:
- many bacteria can enter
- the cow may not be able to protect itself
- bacteria increase in number and spread
- the cow gets mastitis

Cows can also get mastitis because other diseases, such as metritis, pneumonia, digestive problems, or low nutrition, make them weak.

Treatment of mastitis
Treat mastitis as soon as you diagnose it.
Infusion of an antibiotic preparation into the teat canal is the normal treatment.
In acute cases, systemic treatment may be necessary.

Important points:
- penicillin has traditionally been used, but some staphylococci are resistant
- if resistance is suspected, a milk sample should be sent for culture and antibiotic sensitivity testing
- clean, dry, and disinfect the teat before infusion
- do not push the cannula too far into the teat canal
- partial insertion gives better results and reduces damage

How can you treat mastitis?
- call the veterinarian
- the veterinarian may take a milk sample for testing
- antibiotics may be infused into the teat
- antibiotics may sometimes be injected into the muscle or bloodstream if the cow shows poor general condition

Drug residues
Some people are allergic to antibiotics in food.
Because of this, withdrawal times must be strictly respected.

Rules:
- discard milk from all 4 quarters from treated cows
- do not send milk to the collection center for some days after treatment
- do not send the animal for slaughter for some days after treatment
- consult the veterinarian about the exact withdrawal period

Many countries have laws against delivering milk or meat containing antibiotics.

Clean milk utensils
Milking utensils that are not sufficiently clean may contaminate milk.
You must:
- thoroughly clean and disinfect milk cans, buckets, and utensils after use
- keep them in a clean, dust-free, well-ventilated place

How can you prevent mastitis?

Hygiene before milking
- wash dirty buffaloes after wallowing and wash cows if they are dirty
- clean any area where animals are likely to lie down
- clean hands thoroughly before milking
- rinse hands in disinfectant after washing
- if you use a milking machine, clean and disinfect it after each milking and make sure it works correctly

Teat dipping
Udder cleaning before milking is not enough.
After milking, bacteria can remain on the teat.
Teat dipping after every milking is essential to mastitis control.

Before milking
- milk 2 or 3 streams of foremilk from each quarter into a strip cup
- examine carefully
- wash teats in clean or sanitized running water
- dry the teats and udder with disposable paper towel or a clean dry cloth
- use one cloth per cow when possible

During milking
- plan the order of milking
- first milk cows without mastitis
- then cows with suspected mastitis
- finally cows with mastitis
- make sure milking is complete

Dry cow treatment
The best time to treat subclinical mastitis is at the beginning of the dry period, following the last milking.

Advantages:
- cure rate is higher than treatment during lactation
- new infections during the dry period are reduced
- damaged udder tissue has time to recover
- clinical mastitis at the beginning of the next lactation is reduced
- no milk is withdrawn because the cow is dry

Long-acting antibiotic preparations made for intramammary dry cow treatment should not be used during lactation.

After milking
Dip the teats in disinfectant solution, for example:
- hypochlorite solution
- chlorhexidine solution
- iodophor solution

Early detection and early treatment
- look out for changes in milk, udder, and general condition
- treat teat and udder wounds immediately
- treat visible mastitis as soon as possible
- treat hidden mastitis at drying off

Culling
Cull cows:
- with repeated mastitis
- that do not respond to treatment

Checking new animals
Check new animals for mastitis before mixing them with the herd.

Housing
- protect cows from wounds
- avoid short boxes and poor tie systems
- remove manure and dirt
- keep the barn clean
- keep floors dry and provide bedding
- keep flies out
- avoid untrimmed hooves and slippery floors that may injure the udder

Records
Keep records of:
- identity of infected cows
- time of infection
- symptoms
- treatment
- cell counts if available

Preventing mastitis in buffalo
Buffaloes may be more susceptible because of:
- preference for water and muddy places
- dirty milking places
- close contact between healthy and diseased animals
- overcrowding
- poor milking technique
- large pendulous udders and teats prone to injury
- calves causing udder injury
- rough driving and running

How can you prevent mastitis in buffalo?
- wash buffaloes before milking
- use correct milking technique
- if calves suckle, wean them early
- treat udder and teats gently
- do not drive animals roughly or make them run

Important
Injury or damage to the udder leads to mastitis.
Protect the cow from injury and damage.

High-risk periods for mastitis
- early lactation
- just after calving
- beginning and end of the dry period

Just after calving, uterine discharge contains bacteria that can cause mastitis, so the cow and her environment should be kept very clean.
At the beginning and end of the dry period, teat dipping twice a day during the first week after drying off can be useful.
