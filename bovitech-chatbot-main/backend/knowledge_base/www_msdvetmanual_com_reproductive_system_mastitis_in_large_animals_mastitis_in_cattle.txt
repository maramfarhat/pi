TITLE: Mastitis in Cattle
SOURCE: MSD Veterinary Manual

Mastitis in cattle is an inflammation of one or more mammary glands, usually caused by infection when microbes enter through the teat canal.

Main causes
Most infections are caused by:
- Streptococci
- Staphylococci
- Gram-negative bacteria (especially coliforms like E. coli)

Transmission
Two main types:
1. Contagious transmission (during milking)
   - via hands of milkers
   - via milking equipment
   - main pathogens:
     - Staphylococcus aureus
     - Streptococcus agalactiae
     - Corynebacterium bovis

2. Environmental transmission
   - from bedding, water, mud, manure
   - from contaminated equipment
   - from skin lesions or flies

Types of mastitis

1. Subclinical mastitis
- no visible signs
- milk appears normal
- infection detected by high somatic cell count (SCC)

Key points:
- SCC ≥ 200,000 cells/mL → likely infection
- higher SCC → lower milk production
- infections can become chronic and persist long-term

Detection:
- California Mastitis Test (CMT)
- laboratory SCC measurement

2. Clinical mastitis
Visible signs:
- abnormal milk (clots, discoloration)
- swollen, hot, painful udder
- redness

Severity:
- Mild: only local signs
- Moderate: udder inflammation
- Severe:
  - fever
  - loss of appetite
  - shock

Severe mastitis
- often caused by coliform bacteria
- may lead to death or culling
- requires urgent treatment

Signs:
- watery or abnormal milk
- systemic illness
- dehydration

Diagnosis
- milk culture is the most reliable method
- helps identify the causative pathogen

Treatment

General principles:
- identify pathogen if possible
- use appropriate antimicrobials
- respect withdrawal periods

Subclinical mastitis:
- treatment depends on pathogen and duration
- chronic infections (especially S. aureus) are difficult to cure

Clinical mastitis:
- intramammary antibiotics commonly used
- severe cases require:
  - fluids
  - electrolytes
  - anti-inflammatory drugs

Important:
- early treatment improves outcomes
- avoid unnecessary antibiotic use

Severe cases management:
- fluid therapy (very important)
- anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
- sometimes systemic antibiotics

Dry period treatment

The dry period is a key moment:
- higher cure rates
- lower cost
- no milk withdrawal losses

Dry cow therapy:
- intramammary antibiotics at end of lactation
- one tube per quarter

Types:
- Blanket Dry Cow Therapy (all cows)
- Selective Dry Cow Therapy (only infected cows)

Teat sealants:
- create physical barrier
- reduce new infections

Prevention

Milking hygiene:
- wash and dry udders before milking
- use individual towels
- wear gloves
- milk infected cows last

Post-milking teat dipping:
- most important practice
- use disinfectant dip (not spray)

Environment:
- clean, dry bedding
- sand preferred over organic bedding
- regular cleaning

Management:
- avoid overmilking
- maintain milking equipment
- control flies
- prevent teat injuries

Monitoring:
- regular SCC testing
- track infections and cure rates

Key indicators:
- new infection rate < 8%
- cure rate > new infection rate
- SCC < 200,000 cells/mL

Vaccination
- limited effectiveness
- some vaccines reduce severity (e.g., coliform mastitis)

Important notes
- mastitis reduces milk yield and quality
- prevention is more effective than treatment
- chronic cases may require culling

Summary
- mastitis = infection of udder
- caused mainly by bacteria
- can be subclinical or clinical
- detection via SCC and observation
- treatment depends on pathogen
- hygiene is the best prevention
